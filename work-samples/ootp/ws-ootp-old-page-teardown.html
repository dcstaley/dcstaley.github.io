Old Roster Page Teardown: A Rebuild Spec · OOTP Roster Optimizer

Work sample · reverse-engineering an existing surface into a build spec · as of v1 (July 2026)

What this is: a section-by-section teardown of the old app's most complex screen, the roster and lineup page (about 3,000 lines across its components), turned into the shared spec for rebuilding it.

What it shows: reverse-engineering a dense existing surface into a precise inventory of what it does, then separating what to preserve from what to deliberately change.

Source: condensed and edited from the project's teardown document for presentation (trimmed for length, internal references removed). The full working version lives in the app repo.

This is the spec for one screen. It captures what the old page does, the source of truth for behavior, while the rebuild re-architects how, per decisions D4, D5, and D6. It drove the M4 and M5 build, both now complete.

Page layout

The old page is one screen with a drag-and-drop context and several regions: a generation-settings panel with a Generate button; a roster manager pairing a "Next Best Available" pool with the current roster; two lineup editors (vL and vR), each a draggable nine-slot lineup; a pitching-rotation editor and a depth chart; and side panels for cap limits, cap and slots usage, locked cards, and bullpen.

A. Generation config

The old app split generation config across the saved tournament and a separate page-level settings object (the split-config finding). The rebuild decision (D4) merges these into the tournament. The page-level settings that move in: the hitting and pitching metric, the pool sizes (top-X hitters and pitchers), minimum players per position, the owned-cards-only toggle, per-position minimum-rating requirements for both starters and backups, and the position-assignment priority order. Already tournament-sourced: cap and slot rules, eligibility, roster shape, variants, era and park, softcaps, and position weights.

B. Generation behavior

The old page solved one LP several times and layered eight post-processing phases on top: extract, lineup optimization, bench fill, a three-pass catcher routine, locked-card placement, cap reclaim and trim, bonus selection, and a final lineup re-optimization. The rebuild (D5) does this in a single MILP: roster plus dual-lineup assignment, rotation, cap and slots, backup catcher as a coverage-depth constraint, and deterministic cap-reclaim. The multi-phase greedy logic, including the bench-fill value bug and the three-pass catcher, is intentionally retired.

C. Roster manager ("Next Best Available")

A two-pane drag-and-drop: an available-players pool beside the current roster. The pool has eleven need-tabs (hitters vL and vR, owned-only variants, pitchers, qualified starters, infield and outfield range, catcher ability), each showing the top available players sorted by that tab's metric, with variant and base treated as distinct and rostered cards excluded. Each card shows its title, the tab's score, card value, and defensive rating lines. Drag a card onto the roster to add it, respecting the size limit.

D. Lineup editor

Per side, an available-hitters list beside a nine-slot lineup table, with columns for lock, batting order, handedness, player, position, defense, and score. Drag a hitter into a slot to fill it; drag slots to reorder the batting order; a per-slot position dropdown offers the card's eligible positions and swaps when a position is already taken; a lock pins a player to a position so re-optimization respects it. Auto-fill and clear round it out.

E. Side panels

Cap spending limits (the support sub-budgets, folded into the single MILP as optional caps in the rebuild), cap usage, per-tier slots usage with overflow flags, the locked-to-roster list, a depth chart, the pitching-rotation editor, and the bullpen panel.

F. Display details to preserve

Variant rows render with a star and rich title styling; defensive rating lines appear throughout in position-specific formats; and batting order, handedness, and card value show inline.

What changes on purpose (not regressions)

The teardown was explicit about separating faithful reproduction from deliberate improvement, so that intended changes would not later read as regressions:

  • The single MILP replaces the multi-solve and its eight post-processing phases.
  • The three-pass catcher routine becomes a coverage-depth constraint (D5).
  • The page-level settings merge into the tournament (D4).
  • The old cross-pool normalization and power transform are dropped in favor of one honest value scale, with a tunable hitter/pitcher emphasis knob (D2).
