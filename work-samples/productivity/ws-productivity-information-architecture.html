Information Architecture: Unified Tasks and Habits App

Work sample · information architecture · as of v1 (July 2026)

What this is: the app's structure, which screens and surfaces exist and how a user moves between them.

What it shows: structural product design and information architecture.

Source: condensed and edited from the project's information architecture for presentation (trimmed for length, internal references removed). The full working version lives in the app repo.

This document maps a local-first productivity app that unifies tasks and habits in one system. It is a structural artifact written before the wireframes: it settles what the screens are and how they connect, and leaves visual and interaction treatment to a later design pass. Behavior lives in the PRD; this document is about where each surface sits and how it is reached.

How to read this document

This is a structural map, not a visual one. It answers "what are the screens and how do they connect," and leaves "what do they look like and how do controls behave" to the wireframes and the PRD.

Screens are described as one of three structural types:

  • Destination: a place reached from the primary navigation.
  • Sub-surface: a screen that belongs to a destination and is reached from within it.
  • Overlay: a panel, sheet, or dialog that opens over the current view without navigating away. Item create, edit, detail, and all confirmation prompts are overlays per the structural decision below.

Phase tags (Phase 1, 2, 3) match the PRD and roadmap build phases.

Structural decisions

These were decided explicitly and drive the layout below:

  • The product uses a two-dial view model. The Plan surface presents items through a time range (Day, Week, Month) and an organize-by lens (Schedule, Category, Type). Day plus Schedule is the Phase 1 daily planning view.
  • Primary navigation has two destinations: Plan and Progress. Progress is its own destination (Phase 3), not a lens or a settings section.
  • Settings is reached from a persistent icon or menu available everywhere, not from the primary navigation. It opens as an overlay over the current surface, so the user keeps their place, and the main nav stays focused on daily-use surfaces.
  • Item create, edit, and detail, plus Settings, are overlays on the current view, not separate routed screens, so the user keeps their place in the day.
  • The app is usable by guests and works offline. Opening it lands directly on the Plan surface, with no sign-in gate. Authentication is optional and reached from Settings or a dismissible prompt; it turns on sync and backup, it does not gate access.

Zones and access states

The app has one main zone plus an optional auth area. Opening the app lands on the Plan surface and works fully offline; everything (Plan, Progress, Settings, all item surfaces) is available to a guest with no account, and a signed-in user gets the same surfaces plus sync and backup. The auth area (sign in, sign up, verification, password reset) is optional, reached from Settings or a dismissible prompt rather than as a barrier; its only job is to turn on sync.

Three access states, where offline and signed-out are deliberately different things:

  • Guest. No account; data lives only on the device; no sync.
  • Signed in, online. Full app plus live background sync.
  • Signed in, offline. Still signed in, with full local access; sync is paused and reconciles on reconnect. Losing connectivity never signs a user out or drops them to guest.

Only an explicit sign-out changes account state: it confirms data is synced, clears this device's local data, and returns the device to guest. So "no internet" and "signed out" are distinct: the former keeps the user and their data, the latter removes both from the device.

Surface detail

The inventory above says what each surface is; this adds only the structural essentials, how each is reached and how it exits. Full behavior is in the PRD.

Auth area (optional). Sign in and sign up are destinations; email verification, forgot password, and reset password are sub-surfaces reached from them or from an emailed link. Each is reached from Settings or the dismissible sign-up prompt, never as a gate, and each exits back to the app with sync turned on. The OAuth callback is a transient, UI-less handler that exchanges the Google code for a session (linking to an existing account with the same verified email) and returns to the app.

App shell. Wraps every app surface identically for guests and signed-in users. It carries the primary navigation (Plan, and Progress from Phase 3), a persistent Settings affordance, and a sync/offline indicator. The time-range, lens, and date controls belong to the Plan surface, not the shell.

Plan. The primary surface and default landing view, reached with no sign-in gate and fully offline. It holds the two-dial controls (time range and lens), the day's three zones (an all-day band, a time grid, and a "Tasks" column for untimed items), and the item overlays. Item rows open item detail on tap. Later phases add the Category and Type lenses and the Week and Month ranges (the calendar grid is the Schedule lens only; the other lenses render as grouped lists across the span).

Mini-calendar (Plan sub-surface, Phase 3). A collapsible month calendar in a left rail on the Plan surface only, for jumping to a date. Clicking a day moves the Plan view's anchor date without changing the time range, so it is a pure date picker. Its open/closed state is a local device preference, and it defaults open on wide screens and closed on narrow ones.

Plan-level overlays. Item detail, item create/edit, the confirmation prompts (delete, and the optional Google Calendar sync prompts), and the dismissible sign-up prompt all open over the current view so the user keeps their place. None is a separate routed screen.

Progress (Phase 3 destination). A read-only surface listing tracked count-goal habits with their period progress. Logging happens on Plan, not here; both read the same completion records.

Settings (persistent overlay). Reached from anywhere via its icon, opening over the current surface. Its contents grow by phase: account, sync and offline status, and timezone (Phase 1); Google Calendar and tags (Phase 2); default landing view (Phase 3). Sign-out is the only action that clears local data and returns the device to guest.

Key navigation flows

First open (guest, no account): open app to Plan (Day plus Schedule), an empty day grid ready to use, online or offline. No setup, no sign-in step.

Guest decides to back up: Plan or Settings to the sign-up prompt or Account entry, then sign up, verification, and back to the app with sync on and local data migrated.

Returning user on a new device: open app to Settings, sign in, account data syncs down, then the landing view.

Sign in with local guest data and a populated account: sign in raises a one-time "Add the items from this device to your account?" prompt; Add (default) merges the sets, or decline discards the local data after a warning. Signing into an empty account, or with no local guest data, skips this prompt.

Offline use (guest or signed-in): open app (shell cached), and Plan and all surfaces work against the local store; a signed-in user stays signed in and changes sync once a connection returns.

Sign out / switch account: Settings to sign out (confirm synced, warn if unsynced), local data cleared, device returns to guest, then optionally sign into a different account.

Create, edit, or delete an item: Plan to the add affordance (create overlay) or an item row (detail, then edit overlay or delete confirmation), and back to Plan.

Connect Google Calendar (Phase 2): Settings to Google Calendar, connect, Google consent, back to Settings showing connected status.

Wireframe handoffs

The Phase 1 wireframes were produced as HTML mockups, which resolved most of the open structural questions. Resolved there: the day grid plus rail plus all-day band and drag-to-schedule; how a timeframe item reads on the rail; the create/edit overlay and its form factor; completion (toggle plus "Log again" for count habits); the sign-up prompt and a prominent sign-in entry; the sync/offline indicator; the sign-out confirmation; and the color and status rules.

Left for later phases: how tags display in item rows and the Category lens (Phase 2/3); how the Schedule lens renders at the Week and Month ranges and the density of the Month grid (Phase 3); and how the guest-to-account merge prompt and any duplicate cleanup are presented.

Relationship to other documents

The PRD defines what each surface does and how it behaves; this document defines where each surface sits and how it is reached. The technical architecture maps these surfaces to the local-first data layer. The wireframes give each surface its visual form, and the user stories turn each surface and flow into build-ready tickets.
